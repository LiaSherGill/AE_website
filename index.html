We call this tour the Archive Explorer Tour because the stories I'm going to tell you today have all been gleaned from the Tate archive. The archive for the whole of the Tate is here, in the lower levels of Tate Britain, and holds over a million items relating to the history of British art. It includes everything relating to an artists life including letters and sketchbooks as well as more unexpexted items that provide unique insights into the artist such as Francis Bacon's suitcase and model sailing ships belonging to Turner.

Sir Thomas Brock
Sir Henry Tate
1898


What may be surprise you, considering we are in a gallery that bears his name, is that Henry Tate came from humble beginnings. He started work at a young age, around 13, as an apprentice in his brothers green grocers. He then set up a chain of grocer shops himself.

Now, at this time if you wanted some sugar you would go into a green grocers, be met with a young lad much like Henry, who would hack away at a large sugar loaf. You would then take it home, and hack away at it some more. There were two major problems with this process, one, obviously, the sheer inconvenience of it and secondly these sugar loaves often had other substances mixed into them. So the producers could save some money, other substances were mixed in so less pure sugar was used. In the worst cases, the substances were so toxic people died from consuming them.

While Henry is in Germany he comes across this new invention: the sugar cube. He buys a patent for it in the UK, and introduces the sugar cube to this country. You can imagine, what a revelation this would have been, customers could now guarantee that they would be buying pure sugar. And it made Henry's fortune.

What did he decide to do with all this money? Well, he did what a lot of moneyed men of his time did and became a philanthropist: such as giving money to build libraries. He also bought a lot of art. He filled his country home, in Streatham, full of contemporary British art. He would hold show Sunday's, were once a month on a Sunday the public were invited into the house to see his art.

Henry then decided he wanted even more people to see his art so he took his collection to the National Gallery. He asked for one stipulation: that above the door to the gallery it would say "Henry Tate Collection". And what did the National Gallery say? They said no. What was their reasoning? The official response was that the gallery didn't have enough space. However, there are a couple of other reasons why Henry may have been rejected. One, he was a self made man, an entrepreneur. At this time the majority of the art establishment (people who ran the National Gallery) were aristocracy, landed gentry, lords and ladies, from families with lots of wealth, unlike Henry. Another reason, is the type of art he collected. Henry collected contemporary British Art, including the pre-raphaelites, which was not held in high regard at the time. And we're going to find out more on our next stop, the 1840 gallery.

1840 Gallery


Here, in the 1840 gallery we can idea of what it would have been like for visitors who entered the gallery when it opened in 1897. The pantings in here are arranged on the wall in a way that is different to anywhere else in the gallery, and the same way they would have been seen in 1897. This way of arranging the paintings is called a Salon hang, for any of you who have been to the Royal Academy Summer Exhibition you’ll recognise it as it’s still the way they hang the paintings there.

The term Salon hang comes from the French, it was the way paintings were hung in the French art academies, and at this time when people thought of really good art they thought of the French. French and Italian painters were fashionable, not British painters such as those Henry collected and in particular not the pre-raphaelites. An important point to remember about Henry is that he wasn’t an expert in art, he purely collected what he loved not what was fashionable. Paintings such as Millais Ophelia, now the most famous painting in the gallery, was derided when first exhibited.

George Frederic Watts
Hope
1886


Next I want to draw your attention to this painting in the corner, which is mostly overlooked but features in a very dramatic time in Tate history. In the late 1920s the Thames burst it banks. Now if you came in through the main entrance you would have seen how close we are to the river. When the banks burst the river water came rushing into the building and flooded the lower levels of the gallery. At the time these floors held the collection, all the paintings that were not on display. So, in the early hours of the morning there was an emergency rescue effort to save the works of art. Volunteers from the National Gallery and the V&A joined Tate staff in trying to save all they could. Even the director of the gallery, Charles Aitken, came to help out. And as he was wading through the sludge he stepped on something, so he plunged his hand into the water and pulled out this painting, Hope. Now, this would be a wonderful story if it wasn’t for the fact that this painting was Aitken’s least favourite painting in the whole of the collection.

Unlike Aitken, this painting is said to be favourite of Barack Obama and Nelson Mandela. Mandela had a reproduction of the painting with him in his prison cell in Robben Island. When I heard this I thought, it’s an interesting choice, because I looked at it and thought it’s quite a depressing painting isn’t it? But then I started looking a bit closer to try and see what he was seeing in it. The painting depicts the allegorical figure of Hope, she’s on this globe, playing the last string on her lyre and she’s blindfolded, with this gloomy, stormy sky around her. If you look at the very top of the canvas, there’s a tiny yellow spot of paint. A tiny star in this stormy sky. So, even though things seem rock bottom, in the distance there is this tiny star that signals better things to come. The hope that things will get better.

Now, I’ll leave it up to you to decide who you agree with, Charles Aitken or Nelson Mandela. But what this is a great example of is the way in which paintings can evoke different feelings in us.
